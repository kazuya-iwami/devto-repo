### Understanding AWS Resiliency: High Availability, Disaster Recovery, and Continuous Improvement

Before we get into the actual building of this agent, let's first go over some basics of resiliency so that we have a common lexicon as we're going forward.  As we think of resiliency at AWS, we have this mental model broken into three different parts. The first is around high availability. This is the concept of how can our primary application, our active site of our application, withstand common failures that might impact the availability of our service. These are things like a server going down, a network interruption, things like that. If we want our applications to be highly available, we want them to be able to be resilient to these common failures that happen pretty much every day.

The second topic is around disaster recovery. This is when there is a major impact that is influencing our primary site and so we need to fail over to a secondary site. How do we do that? This would be when a disaster hits your primary site of your application where it is no longer able to serve traffic, and so you need to fail over to a secondary location. How quickly are you able to do that, and how are you able to then serve that traffic from your secondary site, and then also fail back over to your primary site once the event is over?

The third component is the continuous improvement of your applications over time. We all know this is true for new features and improving performance, but it is still as critical for resiliency as well. Once you launch an application, it's not done. It's not static. You need to be able to also improve resiliency over time based off of learnings of failures that will happen in your application. So taking the learnings and the hard lessons from failures that you withstand, putting it back into the application, and then just over time as you add new features, new problems can arise and just building over and over, iterating on that to improve it.

### Five Categories of Failures: From Single Points to Shared Fate

With that mental model in mind, we then have different categories of failures that we define. These five categories are those buckets where errors will occur, failures will happen, and so we bucket them into these five categories for ease of identification, but also for remediation. Starting with the first one, most people are familiar with this: single points of failure. So in your design of your application, if there's any component that is by itself or running on a single box, anything like that where if that component were to fail, it brings down your application and you have a bad day. When we're looking at this, we're looking at how do we expand that single point of failure to be not a single point, but have redundancy in it so that it can withstand any individual failure.

 Next is excessive load, so looking at is our application able to withstand a sudden increase in load.

This could be anything from making sure you can scale elastically, either ahead of the load or in response to the load, and also making sure that you have the headroom, whether it be service limits within AWS or whatever capacity you might need to start to accept and handle that traffic.

 Next is around excessive latency. Is our application built to withstand any kind of network interruptions? Are the timeouts that we're building within our code able to accept variations where sometimes it's 5 milliseconds, sometimes it's 200 milliseconds?
