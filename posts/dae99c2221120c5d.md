 I wanted to address a very common misconception about quantum computing right from the start. A quantum computer is not a classical computer that just runs a little bit faster. In fact, quantum computers are actually very slow compared to the clock cycles of classical computers. Instead, a quantum computer can run a richer class of algorithms compared to classical computers. You may ask why we need a richer class of algorithms when we have classical algorithms that work just fine. Well, it turns out there are certain types of applications where all the algorithms that we know require exponential overhead as the problem gets bigger. The time needed, the memory needed, and other resources needed all grow exponentially.

[![Thumbnail 70](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/kazuya-iwami/devto-repo/main/posts/images/dae99c2221120c5d/70.jpg)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xqb5i8RxmwE&t=70)

 One of the prototypical problems of that kind is simulating quantum systems themselves. This is the home turf application of quantum computers. When we look at a system with particles like electrons that interact with each other and behave quantum mechanically, simulating them with a classical computer becomes extremely challenging. Already 50 of those particles require supercomputing-scale classical computing. Because the scaling is exponential, if we go from 50 to 100 particles, it becomes completely out of reach for any computer today and in the future. This idea is almost the founding idea of quantum computing and goes back a long time now.

[![Thumbnail 110](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/kazuya-iwami/devto-repo/main/posts/images/dae99c2221120c5d/110.jpg)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xqb5i8RxmwE&t=110)

 This concept goes back to 1981 with Richard Feynman's very famous quote where he said, "Nature isn't classical, and if you want to make a simulation of nature, then you better make it quantum mechanical. And by golly, it's a wonderful problem because it doesn't look so easy." It's actually a very hard problem to build a quantum computer. The technology is still early, but we're seeing a lot of improvements.

[![Thumbnail 170](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/kazuya-iwami/devto-repo/main/posts/images/dae99c2221120c5d/170.jpg)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xqb5i8RxmwE&t=170)

 The take-home message is that quantum computers can solve some problems with exponentially fewer gates or fewer operations than classical computers because they use different types of algorithms. To put it provocatively, it's not a two times speed-up, but a two to the power of X speed-up. Because it's exponential, that's the difference between something that's possible and something that's not possible to compute.

[![Thumbnail 190](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/kazuya-iwami/devto-repo/main/posts/images/dae99c2221120c5d/190.jpg)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xqb5i8RxmwE&t=190)

 From this brief introduction, there are actually some very profound learnings and lessons we can take. Because quantum computers are not just a classical computer that runs faster, it means that quantum will not replace classical computers. If you have a classical algorithm that does not scale exponentially and works well for the problem you have, there's no reason why you would replace it with a quantum computer because they're generally slower and generally more expensive.

[![Thumbnail 210](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/kazuya-iwami/devto-repo/main/posts/images/dae99c2221120c5d/210.jpg)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xqb5i8RxmwE&t=210)

 The second point is that many use cases of quantum computers are hidden by definition. If you look around at your company and think about what applications a quantum computer could be used for, chances are there are some applications you don't see because they are not possible to solve with classical computers today. By definition, we want to apply quantum computing to problems that are hard for classical computers. The final point is that because we're talking about a new class of algorithms, these applications are not just lift and shift. When we have a quantum computer at some point in the future that can address industry-relevant problems, we cannot just take what we were running on a classical computer before and move it over to a quantum computer and have it work. These are completely new algorithms, completely new primitives, and it requires a lot of talent, skills, and intellectual property to develop these kinds of algorithms. We're very excited about these algorithms.
