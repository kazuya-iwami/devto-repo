### AWS Site-to-Site VPN Fundamentals and Common Connectivity Patterns

 Coming to the basics of Site-to-Site VPN, AWS Site-to-Site VPN is a fully managed service which allows you to connect your remote sites to the workloads in AWS. The way it works is that on your on-premises site, you have your gateway devices which connect to one of the gateways on the AWS side. That gateway could be a Virtual Private Gateway, Transit Gateway, or a Cloud WAN core network. Whenever you establish a Site-to-Site VPN connection, we provision two tunnels, and both these tunnel endpoints live in different availability zones for high availability reasons.

Since this is a managed service, you don't need to worry about any patching and tunnel maintenance work, and the service takes care of it. Due to this reason, we highly recommend that whenever customers are using Site-to-Site VPN connections, they use both tunnels so that in case of any traffic interruption on one tunnel, you can still use the other tunnel as a backup.

Now, let's look at some of the common connectivity patterns in terms of how customers connect their sites using Site-to-Site VPN. The most simple and straightforward is using Virtual Private Gateway, or VGW, to connect your remote sites to AWS, and this is specifically helpful if your deployment pattern is pretty simple. What I mean by that is if you need to connect your sites only to a single VPC in AWS, Virtual Private Gateway is very simple and handy, especially because your routing needs are going to be simple. But when it comes to complex routing needs and you need to connect multiple VPCs to your remote sites, that is when Transit Gateway and Cloud WAN come in handy.

 VPN Concentrator is a feature specifically geared towards use cases that require customers to connect a large number of remote sites to access their applications or workloads in AWS. ### 5 Gbps VPN Tunnel: Meeting High-Throughput Requirements

Moving to the next topic, the 5 Gbps tunnel. This is interesting. So far, we were talking about VPN concentrators for connecting remote sites that have low bandwidth requirements. The 5 Gbps tunnel is actually the opposite end of the spectrum, where we have customers who found the 1.25 Gbps VPN connection insufficient given the nature of their workload. What ends up happening today is that many of our customers use site-to-site VPN in conjunction with Direct Connect.  Either as a backup or running site-to-site VPN connections over your Direct Connect. Some of you might be familiar that Direct Connect provides higher throughput, such as 5 Gbps, 10 Gbps, or even 10+ Gbps throughput.

What ended up happening was that because site-to-site VPN tunnels could only do 1.25 Gbps, customers had to rely on using the ECMP protocol. The ECMP protocol helped you by enabling it on your transit gateway so that all the VPN connections attached to your transit gateway were logically bound together, allowing you to get higher throughput and exceed your 1.25 Gbps per tunnel limit. This works when you have fewer VPN connections or fewer Direct Connects, but eventually it started adding significant operational overhead and complexity for customers to maintain. One other observation we had was that ECMP, while it works well in most cases, may not always deliver consistent bandwidth performance depending on highly latency-sensitive workloads or highly data-intensive workloads.

[![Thumbnail 1340](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/kazuya-iwami/devto-repo/main/posts/images/d45bc540f1e3cc8d/1340.jpg)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bt5IwPVnSnw&t=1340)

The 5 Gbps tunnel support is aimed at solving this problem so that ideally you don't have to use, or at least reduces the need to use, protocols like ECMP to get higher bandwidth. One thing to be mindful about the 5 Gbps tunnel is that when you create a VPN connection, you have two options to select your bandwidth.  The default or standard option is still your regular 1.25 Gbps VPN connection. If you are interested in using the 5 Gbps VPN connection or 5 Gbps tunnel, you select the large option. From a configuration perspective, everything remains the same. The only thing that has changed is the new addition of the tunnel bandwidth parameter.

[![Thumbnail 1400](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/kazuya-iwami/devto-repo/main/posts/images/d45bc540f1e3cc8d/1400.jpg)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bt5IwPVnSnw&t=1400)

One thing to keep in mind is that you cannot change the tunnel bandwidth for your existing connection. What I mean by that is if you have provisioned a 1.25 Gbps VPN today and want to move to a 5 Gbps VPN, you need to first create a 5 Gbps VPN connection, migrate your existing connection to that, and then finally delete your old 1.25 Gbps VPN connection. This is applicable both ways, so you cannot move from 5 Gbps to 1.25 Gbps VPN either.  In terms of feature parity between your 1.25 Gbps VPN connections and 5 Gbps VPN connections, it is pretty much the same. There are just two main differences. One is that accelerated VPN is not supported with your 5 Gbps tunnel, but it is supported with your 1.25 Gbps tunnel. The other thing is in terms of the gateways supported, it is only supported on Transit Gateway and Cloud WAN. It is not supported on Virtual Gateway. Other than that, all the features are supported for both tunnels, including ECMP.

[![Thumbnail 1500](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/kazuya-iwami/devto-repo/main/posts/images/d45bc540f1e3cc8d/1500.jpg)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bt5IwPVnSnw&t=1500)

I know we talked about the ECMP protocol and the inability to move between 1.25 and 5 Gbps VPN connections. But what you can do if needed is use ECMP between your 1.25 Gbps VPNs and 5 Gbps VPNs. The way it would work is that imagine you have a transit gateway with ECMP enabled, and you have one VPN which is 1.25 Gbps and another VPN which is 5 Gbps. In that case, you can logically bind them together to get higher throughput. For any additional considerations, you can refer to the blog post, which talks about how you can deploy the 5 Gbps VPN connections. So next time when you have higher throughput requirements, I hope you no longer have to rely on ECMP to get that and can directly use the 5 Gbps tunnel.
